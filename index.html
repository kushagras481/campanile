01 · Abstract

Physically Accurate Light,
In Real Time

We present a real-time, browser-based renderer of UC Berkeley's Campanile (Sather Tower) driven by physically accurate lighting simulation. Rather than relying on hand-tuned artistic values, every visual parameter — sky chromaticity, shadow direction and length, surface reflectance, rain, fog, and night lighting — derives from physical or astronomical models.

The renderer exposes an interactive time-of-day slider, a day-of-year slider for seasonal variation, and selectable weather presets (clear, overcast, rain). The scene runs entirely in the browser via Three.js and WebGL 2.0, requiring no installation. The Campanile geometry was modeled in Blender with named architectural sub-components (pilasters, belfry shaft, windows) and rendered at 60 fps on consumer hardware.

Key technical contributions include a GPU Preetham sky shader parameterized by true solar azimuth and altitude computed from Berkeley's GPS coordinates, PCF soft shadow mapping driven by the live solar position, a dry/wet BRDF material system for the stone tower, a rain particle system with atmospheric scattering tints, exponential fog with time-of-day density variation, and a procedural lightning system. The result is a single interactive scene that authentically captures Sather Tower across dawn, noon, dusk, clear nights, overcast mornings, and thunderstorms.

02 · Technical Approach

Systems & Methods

The renderer is structured as a set of loosely coupled subsystems, each responsible for one physical phenomenon. All subsystems are synchronized each frame to a common time-of-day and date, ensuring visual coherence across lighting, sky, shadows, weather, and materials.

01
Solar Positioning

Sun azimuth and altitude are computed from first principles using standard solar geometry equations, anchored to Berkeley's GPS coordinates (37.87°N, 122.26°W) with a longitude correction and DST offset. The resulting unit vector drives the directional light, shadow camera frustum, and sky shader every frame.

$$\delta = 23.45° \,\sin\!\left(\tfrac{360°\,(284 + N)}{365}\right)$$ $$\sin\alpha = \sin\phi\,\sin\delta + \cos\phi\,\cos\delta\,\cos h$$
N = day-of-year, φ = latitude, h = hour angle, δ = declination, α = altitude.

The single-sine declination model is a deliberate simplification of the full NOAA SPA — sufficient for an interactive seasonal slider, validated to within ~1° at solstice noon and equinox sunrise.

02
Preetham Sky Shader

A custom GPU sky dome renders the full atmosphere using the Preetham et al. (1999) analytical sky model. The fragment shader evaluates the per-pixel luminance distribution from the angle to the zenith and the angle to the sun, modulated by turbidity:

$$F(\theta, \gamma) = (1 + A\,e^{B / \cos\theta})\,(1 + C\,e^{D\gamma} + E\,\cos^2\gamma)$$
θ = angle from zenith, γ = angle from sun, A–E = turbidity-dependent coefficients (we use T = 2.5 for clear, T = 8 for storms).

A procedural cloud layer is amplitude-modulated by two incommensurate sines on the CPU, $c(t) = c_0 + 0.04\,\sin(0.13\,t) + 0.03\,\sin(0.07\,t)$, adding gentle sub-perceptual "breathing" that kills the static feel of a fixed cloud-cover value.

03
Shadow Mapping

A 2048×2048 PCF soft shadow map is produced by a directional light whose position and frustum track the live solar direction. Shadow bias and normal bias are tuned to suppress Peter Panning at low sun elevations. Cloud cover continuously modulates shadow intensity — reducing it by up to 25% under full overcast — to match the soft, directionless nature of diffuse cloud lighting.

04
Campanile Geometry

The Campanile mesh was hand-modeled in Blender as a set of named architectural sub-components (pilasters, belfry shaft, window frames, pyramid roof) and exported as a single glTF binary. The mesh is scaled to approximately the tower's real-world height (~94 m). A MeshStandardMaterial provides physically based rendering, with roughness modulated procedurally per-fragment to simulate stone grain, masonry courses, and weathering streaks rather than relying on prebaked texture maps.

05
Weather & Atmosphere

Three weather presets (clear, overcast, rain) smoothly interpolate fog color, fog density, cloud cover, turbidity, and rain strength. Exponential fog density is multiplicatively boosted near dawn and dusk to mimic morning mist:

$$\sigma(t) = \sigma_{\text{base}} \cdot \mathrm{lerp}\!\left(1.5,\, 1.0,\, \mathrm{smoothstep}(s_y;\, 0.05,\, 0.4)\right)$$
σ = fog density, s_y = vertical component of sun direction. Density falls from 1.5× to 1× as the sun climbs above the horizon.

The fog color is dimmed with the sun's elevation so nighttime fog reads dark rather than the daytime horizon color. A wet BRDF mode lowers stone roughness with the wetness scalar w ∈ [0, 1]:

$$r(w) = (1 - w)\,r_{\text{dry}} + w\,r_{\text{wet}} = 0.85 - 0.60\,w$$
r_dry = 0.85 (matte stone), r_wet = 0.25 (rain-slicked, near-specular).
06
Rain & Lightning

Rain is rendered as a particle system of short streak billboards, tinted in real time by the scene's live fog color so precipitation integrates atmospherically into any lighting condition. A procedural lightning system fires stochastically during storms, boosting sky exposure, hemisphere light intensity, and rain streak brightness for a single-frame flash. Moonlight is modeled as a dim cool directional light placed opposite the sun, fading in at night and out under cloud cover.

Implementation Stack

The project uses Three.js r165 over WebGL 2.0, bundled with Vite and deployed via GitHub Pages. The sky pipeline uses custom GLSL shaders authored in .vert.glsl / .frag.glsl files imported as raw strings; the stone material is patched via Three.js's onBeforeCompile hook to inject procedural weathering, masonry, and wet-state interpolation into the standard PBR shader. The UI panel is built with Tweakpane — time of day, day of year, weather preset, shadow toggle, and lightning toggle are all live-bound to the render loop.

Performance

The scene maintains 60 fps at 1440p on an Apple M2 MacBook Pro (GPU utilization ~35%). The main cost is the 2048² shadow map update, which occurs every frame to track the sun. A dynamic environment map is re-rendered each frame at low resolution to provide approximate reflections on wet stone without a full ray-traced global illumination pass.

04 · Lessons Learned

What We Discovered

  • 1 Physical models expose their seams at extreme conditions. The Preetham sky model — which we validated against reference chromaticity charts at T = 2.5 — produces visible banding near the sun disk at very low elevations (below 2°). This is a known limitation of the analytical approximation; a full implementation would upgrade to the Hosek-Wilkie (2012) model for twilight. The lesson: validate early and at boundary conditions, not just at the "comfortable" midday case.
  • 2 Shadow map tuning is empirical, not derivable. Bias parameters for shadow mapping do not have closed-form solutions — they depend on mesh scale, light angle, and map resolution in ways that require iteration. We spent significant time at low sun elevations suppressing Peter Panning without introducing shadow acne, ultimately combining depth bias, normal bias, and a 25% shadow intensity reduction under overcast. The takeaway: budget time for empirical tuning of any numerical parameter in real-time rendering.
  • 3 Procedural materials beat baked textures for iteration speed. Rather than authoring and re-baking roughness/diffuse texture maps for the stone, we patched Three.js's standard PBR shader via onBeforeCompile to inject sin-noise stone grain, masonry course bands, and a wet-state interpolation directly in the fragment shader. Tuning weathering and the dry/wet transition became a matter of editing a few constants and reloading — no bake step, no UV re-unwrap, no re-export. The takeaway: for stylized PBR where artistic control matters more than photographic fidelity, procedural shader injection is dramatically more iterable than a texture-map pipeline.
  • 4 Perceptual coherence requires atmospheric coupling across subsystems. Rain looked disconnected from the scene until we tinted the streak billboards with the live fog color — a small change that made precipitation feel like part of the atmosphere rather than an overlay. Similarly, nighttime fog looked wrong until its brightness was modulated by solar elevation. Physically correct individual systems do not automatically compose into a perceptually correct whole; the coupling between systems matters as much as their individual accuracy.
  • 5 Browser-based deployment is both a strength and a constraint. Running entirely in the browser with no installation removed every barrier to sharing and demonstrating the project. However, the WebGL API's lack of compute shaders (in WebGL 2.0) and the browser's memory pressure model constrained our particle count and texture resolution. A native OpenGL or Vulkan backend would have allowed volumetric ray marching for clouds and higher-resolution shadow maps, both of which remain on the stretch-goal list.
